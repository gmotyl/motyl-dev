NeoKim just announced the launch of Part 2 of their Generative AI Masterclass, starting in 24 hours. If you've been curious about how companies build their own AI assistants instead of relying on generic chatbots, this might be worth your attention.

## What's the deal

This is a step-by-step masterclass delivered via newsletter. Each edition builds on the previous one, so you're not just learning "how to use AI" - you're supposed to understand how to design, build, and ship your own assistant.

The program covers the full stack: from how chat messages actually work under the hood (tokenization, prefill, decode phases, KV-cache) through to building a production-minded assistant with streaming, system prompts, JSON output, and persistent memory.

## What you'll actually learn

The curriculum breaks down into six main areas:

**How chat works** - Tokenization, prefill, decode phases, KV-cache, and why longer conversations get slower and more expensive.

**How the model got there** - Pretraining, post-training (SFT and RLHF), and scaling laws.

**Output controls** - Temperature, top-p, top-k, why hallucination happens, and which settings to use for which task.

**Context engineering** - System prompts, few-shot, chain-of-thought, structured outputs, context window management, and persistent memory.

**Cost and evaluation** - Token math across model tiers, prompt caching, model routing, golden test sets, and LLM-as-Judge evaluation.

**Practical build** - Eight steps from a single API call to a production assistant.

## Why I care

As someone who's been building with LLMs for a while, the most valuable part here seems to be the practical eight-step build. Most tutorials give you an API call and stop there. Actually shipping something with streaming, proper prompt injection defense, and persistent memory - that's the hard part that nobody talks about.

The cost and evaluation section also catches my eye. Most developers I know have no idea how to tell if their AI is actually working well. LLM-as-Judge is an interesting approach to that problem.

## Worth your time?

If you're a developer who's played with ChatGPT and wants to understand how to build something similar, this looks like a structured way to learn it. The "learn by building" approach is solid. Whether it's worth the price depends on how much you value having it all organized in one place versus just piecing together docs and tutorials.

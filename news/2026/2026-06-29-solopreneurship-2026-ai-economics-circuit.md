## Reflections on Solopreneurship in 2026

**TLDR:** A seven-year solopreneur who trained transformer models back in 2019 just took their first corporate job. They break down why AI is compressing margins for independent workers even as solopreneurship itself is booming in the US.

**Summary:** Seven years is a long time to build something on your own. The author started in 2019 training transformer models for a mobile chatbot, and last week they walked into an office as an employee for the first time in nearly a decade. I find this fascinating not because it's a failure story, but because it's an honest accounting of a structural shift that most "build your empire" content deliberately ignores.

The US numbers are genuinely striking. Out of 523,971 new business applications filed in May 2026, only about 6% were projected to become employer businesses. The rest are solo operations. And roughly 89% of those solo businesses have adopted AI tools. Four million Americans were earning primary income as solopreneurs at $100K+ annually as far back as 2023, up from the low millions in the early 2010s. The number crossing $1M revenue doubled between 2023 and 2025. On paper, this looks like a golden age.

But the Upwork data tells a different story at the margin level. Writing demand down 32% year-over-year in 2025. IT and networking down 27%. Web and mobile dev down 13%. Yes, AI-category demand grew 50%, but the total number of active buyers peaked in 2023 and has been declining since. Upwork's stock sits 86% below its 2021 peak. The same pattern on Fiverr. What's happening is that AI is creating new demand categories while simultaneously destroying the purchasing appetite for everything else. The pie is changing shape faster than most people can pivot.

The author is refreshingly self-critical about their own arc. They spent time building in too many directions: Gumroad, YouTube, Substack, paid ads, an AI filmmaking studio called Project Skia. The core insight they land on is that for an AI specialist, the growth bottleneck shifted from building deep expertise to doing sales and marketing. That's a genuinely uncomfortable observation. The moat used to be skills. Now the durable moat is clients plus distribution plus domain expertise plus operational efficiency. If your primary differentiator is "I know AI," you're competing with everyone, including the models themselves.

The European contrast is sharp. Self-employment in the Netherlands actually declined in 2025 for the first time in 13 years. EU-wide, self-employment sits at 14.3%, down from 15% in 2010. The author took a job at ML6, a European AI consultancy, as a domain CTO. That's not a retreat. That's a strategic move into a structure that provides the distribution and client relationships that solo operations struggle to build.

There's also a brief but interesting news roundup at the end of the piece. OpenAI previewed GPT-5.6 as a three-tier model family (Sol, Terra, Luna) in limited API access. Sakana AI launched Fugu, an orchestration layer that routes across Claude, GPT, and Gemini behind a single endpoint, claiming their premium tier beats Claude Opus 4.8 on SWE-Bench Pro. Zhipu's GLM-5.2, a 744B-parameter open-weight coding model under MIT license, has practitioners moving routine coding work onto it at roughly 5x lower cost than Opus. And Mozilla's autonomous agent harness, built around Claude, found a 15-year-old Firefox security bug and contributed to 423 security fixes in a single month. The Mozilla story is the most technically interesting of the bunch because the engineers are clear: the win came from the harness architecture, not the model. File scoring, goal loops, a browser evaluator, and a verifier sub-agent. That's the actual engineering.

**Key takeaways:**
- Solopreneurship is booming in the US by volume but margin compression is real, with major freelance platforms seeing declining buyer counts since 2022-2023
- For AI specialists, the bottleneck has shifted from technical skill to distribution and client acquisition, which are structurally harder for solos to build
- The durable formula is deep expertise plus AI plus owned distribution plus direct client relationships
- Europe is moving in the opposite direction from the US on self-employment trends
- GLM-5.2 (MIT-licensed, 744B MoE) is seeing real adoption as a lower-cost Claude alternative for routine coding tasks
- Mozilla's Firefox bug-finding success came from harness architecture, not model capability alone

**Why do I care:** This piece matters because it's one of the few honest assessments of where AI is actually landing in the freelance and solopreneur economy. From a senior engineering perspective, the Mozilla harness story is the most actionable insight: the model is a commodity component. The architecture around it, how you score files, loop on goals, verify outputs, is where the engineering leverage lives. If you're building AI tooling or thinking about your own positioning, the author's formula is worth stress-testing against your own situation. "If everything is AI, what even is AI?" is the right question to be asking right now.

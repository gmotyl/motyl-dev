## Borys Had the Best Dribbling — On Shipping, Perfectionism, and Getting Out of Your Own Way

**TLDR:** Oskar Dudycz revisits a personal essay he wrote over ten years ago about why smart, well-prepared engineers consistently fail to ship. The culprit is a familiar one: obsessing over foundations while the actual goal gathers dust. The essay weaves in Amy Hoy's "Just Fucking Ship" as a counterweight, and the message holds up surprisingly well in the age of AI-assisted everything.

**Summary:**

There is something genuinely rare about a technical writer who leads with a schoolyard football memory instead of a class diagram. Oskar opens with Borys, the kid who had the best dribbling in 5B, and uses that as a lens for a much older question: why do technically capable people so rarely finish what they start?

The essay is autobiographical and honest in a way that most engineering writing refuses to be. Oskar names the pattern plainly. He is the guy who reads the instructions, sets up auction snipers, researches everything thoroughly, and then builds impeccable architecture for products that never reach users. Solid SOLID principles. Beautiful internals. An iceberg nobody sails past.

Amy Hoy's book enters as a practical corrective. The argument she makes, and that Oskar amplifies, is that we invert the order of operations. We choose the domain name before we write a single line that matters. We burn peak motivation on scaffolding. We postpone the actual work until the conditions are perfect, and perfect conditions never arrive. The dinner party analogy lands: when friends are coming over, you adjust the menu to fit the time, you do not cancel dinner because you cannot make a three-course meal from scratch.

What strikes me most revisiting this is how durable the diagnosis is. In 2026, with generative AI making it trivially easy to generate boilerplate, set up frameworks, and produce the appearance of progress, the trap has only gotten deeper. It is easier than ever to look busy at the starting line. The essay was written before LLMs were a daily tool, and yet the sentence "we're overfocused on doing rather than shipping stuff that matters" reads like it was written last week.

Oskar closes with a note that he learned to swim and signed up for a swimming competition. That is the point. The medal matters, however it comes. At 40, reflecting on words he wrote at 29, he is not embarrassed by the essay. He is still working on the same problem. That kind of honesty is worth more than a perfectly formatted technical post.

**Key takeaways:**
- Set the goal and the deadline first, then figure out the scope and methods, not the other way around
- Peak motivation spent on infrastructure and setup is motivation that never reaches the thing users actually need
- Small, achievable milestones matter more than a grand vision that paralyzes you at the starting line
- The GenAI era makes it easier to generate scaffolding and harder to avoid confusing activity with progress
- Shipping something imperfect beats maintaining a perfect unfinished project indefinitely

**Why do I care:** As someone who thinks about systems and architecture every day, this essay is an uncomfortable mirror. The instinct to get the foundations right before building is not wrong, it is just dangerously easy to abuse. In frontend and backend work alike, the architecture astronaut tendency costs real time and real user value. The discipline of shipping, of constraining scope to fit a deadline rather than expanding scope to fill ambition, is genuinely one of the harder professional skills to develop. This essay does not solve that problem, but it names it clearly, and that is more useful than another framework.
